About Us

The shortage of tomatoes due to reduced productivity is a pressing concern in the agricultural landscape, not only in India but globally. The annual conditions of tomato crops in India exhibit a fluctuating pattern influenced by a multitude of factors, including climate variability, pest and disease pressures, and farming practices. This volatility often results in fluctuations in tomato yields, impacting the supply chain and causing shortages in the market. India is one of the largest producers of tomatoes in the world, with a significant portion of its agricultural sector dependent on tomato cultivation. However, variations in climate, such as unseasonal rainfall or extreme temperatures, can lead to crop damage and reduced yields. Additionally, challenges related to pest and disease management, post-harvest losses, and inadequate infrastructure for cold storage and transportation further contribute to the shortage of tomatoes. Addressing these issues requires a multi-faceted approach that encompasses research and development, sustainable farming practices, improved access to technology, and investments in infrastructure to enhance productivity and reduce supply chain inefficiencies.


India is the world’s second largest tomato producer after China and currently accounts for 11% of the total global tomato production. India, produces over 20 million tonnes (in 2022) of tomato grown on 8.4 lakh hectares with an average productivity of around 24.5 tonnes per hectare.The majority of the tomatoes produced in the country are consumed fresh with the remaining being processed into products such as tomato paste, tomato juice, tomato sauce, tomato ketchup, etc.

Tomato Wastage

According to a report by the Ministry of Food Processing Industries, India wastes around 16% of its total tomato production every year, which amounts to approximately 7.5 million tonnes of tomatoes. The main reasons for tomato wastage in India are Poor infrastructure and lack of storage facilities, Inefficient supply chain management, Lack of market access.

Pest and disease prevention is a critical aspect of successful tomato cultivation. Tomatoes are susceptible to various pests and diseases that can lead to reduced yields or even the loss of an entire crop if not managed effectively.

  • Identifying Common Pests and Diseases: Aphids: These tiny, pear-shaped insects cluster on the undersides of leaves and suck plant sap, causing wilting and stunted growth.
  • Early Detection: Regularly inspect your tomato plants for signs of pests and diseases. Catching problems early makes them easier to manage.
  • Proper Spacing: Plant your tomatoes with adequate spacing to allow for good air circulation, which can help prevent fungal diseases
  • Mulching: Use organic mulch, like straw or compost, around your tomato plants to deter weeds and reduce soil splash, which can spread diseases.

Common diseases affecting tomato plants include early blight, late blight, powdery mildew, and bacterial wilt. They can be identified by characteristic symptoms like leaf spots, white powdery growth, and wilting. Monitoring involves regular visual inspections, utilizing disease-resistant tomato varieties, and implementing proper crop rotation and sanitation practices to reduce disease pressure.

Common pests posing a significant threat to tomato crops include aphids, whiteflies, hornworms, and tomato fruitworms. Effective monitoring involves using sticky traps, pheromone traps, and regular visual inspections. Integrated pest management (IPM) strategies, such as introducing beneficial insects or using organic pesticides when necessary, can help manage these pests while minimizing environmental impact.

Early warning signs of disease and pest infestations in tomato crops include wilting or yellowing of leaves, small holes or spots on leaves, visible pests such as aphids or caterpillars, and the presence of discolored or misshapen fruit. Timely recognition of these symptoms can help initiate prompt intervention to mitigate damage.

Advanced disease and pest monitoring tools in tomato cultivation include remote sensing using drones or satellites to detect stress patterns, automated sensor systems for data collection, and smartphone apps that can identify diseases and pests through image recognition. These technologies enhance precision and efficiency in monitoring, allowing for quicker responses to threats.

The best practices for maintaining a disease and pest monitoring program in a tomato farm throughout the growing season include regular scouting of plants for symptoms, implementing traps and pheromone lures, keeping detailed records, and staying updated on local pest and disease trends to adjust strategies as needed. Timely intervention based on monitoring results is critical for effective pest and disease management.
